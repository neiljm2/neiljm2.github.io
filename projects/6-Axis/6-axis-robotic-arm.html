6-Axis Robotic Arm from Scratch

Design, fabrication, and custom multi-scheme controls implementation

Project Overview

This project involved building a custom 6-axis robotic arm from the ground up, starting from structural design in CAD to routing motor controller boards and writing real-time control algorithms. The primary goal was to navigate the end-effector smoothly through a constrained space.

Figure 1: Initial mechanical assembly of the robotic arm structural linkages before routing electrical systems.

Mechanical Design & Assembly

The arm was modeled in SolidWorks, factoring in structural strength, rotational limits, and weight distribution. The linkages were custom 3D-printed using robust infill patterns and reinforced with custom aluminum support brackets to withstand dynamic loads.

Figure 2: Wiring harness assembly and breadboarding of stepper drivers during prototype testing.

Electrical Integration

Wiring up a 6-axis manipulator requires careful cable routing to prevent binding and wire strain during motion. Custom power buses and driver boards were wired to step down the supply voltage and isolate logic control lines from motor drivers.

Figure 3: Cleaned custom motor leads and cable bundles routed through joint sleeves.

Cable Management Optimization

To ensure reliability, the temporary prototyping wires were replaced with structured cable bundles. Using braided sleeves and custom-soldered leads routed through the hollow axes of the joints, the cable management was optimized to minimize rotational resistance.

Figure 4: First dynamic test run demonstrating stepper rotation speeds and joint limits.

First Motion Calibration

With hardware and electrical systems finalized, the initial controller code was loaded to check basic actuator functionality. The first motion tests revealed rotational offsets and inertial jitter, which were mitigated by introducing acceleration profiling.

Figure 5: Coordinated joint movement testing demonstrating multi-axis velocity synchronization.

Control Loop Tuning

To coordinate complex, multi-joint trajectories, a joint-space control loop was implemented. By reading real-time encoder values, the microcontroller synchronized the stepper frequencies dynamically, enabling smoother arcs and path tracking.

Figure 6: Custom-designed end-effector gripper executing a simple pick-and-place task.

End-Effector Integration

The final link of the arm is a custom-actuated gripper designed to pick and place objects. Servos drive the grabber plates, requiring fine-grained task-space controls to coordinate cartesian end-effector positioning with the gripper actuation states.

Figure 7: The robotic manipulator smoothly completing its trajectory path without drift or collision.

Trajectory Optimization & Success

By combining joint-space moves, cartesian task-space pathing, and customized feedback regulation, the arm achieved stable path tracking accuracy. The trajectory optimization solved the inverse kinematics solver loop in real-time, completing the physical course.

Figure 8: Coordinated speed test illustrating complete control stability at accelerated velocities.

Performance Evaluation

Finally, the manipulator was tested under high speed and loaded conditions to locate physical stress limits. The system maintained dynamic tracking stability and structural integrity, proving the feasibility of custom low-cost robot manipulator designs.
